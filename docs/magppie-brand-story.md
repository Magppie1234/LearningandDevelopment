## Part One — The concept of Material Science

Nature Never Make Mistakes.
Every material in nature is scientific, each one carries a unique composition, unique properties, unique capabilities and a unique purpose that make it ideal for specific applications. Every material is not suitable for every application.

For example,

1. Copper carries electricity, steel does not.
2. But steel carries structural strength.
3. Mirror can reflect our image, glass cannot.
4. But glass carries transparency.
5. Wood can lit fire, stone cannot lit fire.
6. Stone withstands heat, water, pressure and time.

Every material is born with its own properties. And for thousands of years, humanity has advanced by understanding one simple principle:

The right material in the right place creates the best performance.

When we choose the wrong material in any application, sooner it later, it will show its worst effects.

For example:
1. A car cannot be made from wood.
2. A bridge cannot be built from paper.
3. An electrical system cannot run efficiently on cotton threads.

Every engineer understands this principle. That is why electrical systems around the world use copper.

Copper carries extraordinary conductivity, allowing electricity to travel efficiently and safely through homes, buildings, smartphones, computers, hospitals, and factories.

Imagine replacing copper with iron. No electricity can be generated.

Replace it with wood. The system would not work at all.

Nature has already chosen the correct materials for all applications.

Our responsibility is simply to understand it and respect it.

## Part Two — Why we ignored this Material Science inside our homes?

Surprisingly, while engineering has become highly sophisticated in automobiles, aircraft, electronics and medicine, the same scientific thinking has mostly been absent inside our homes, which happens to be the living space for our families.

Particularly in the most important room of all:

The Kitchen.

The kitchen is not furniture. The kitchen is a food preparation laboratory.

It is exposed daily to: fire, steam, water, oil, heat, acids, heavy usage, food contamination risks.

In many homes, the kitchen operates for 10 to 14 hours every day, preparing three meals of the day.

The health of an entire family begins here.

Yet the material most commonly used worldwide for kitchen cabinets is compressed wood boards, particle boards, MDF, HDF, HDHMR boards, and plywood.

These materials are originally created from wood fibers, wood waste, glue, chemicals and resins.

This raises an important question:

Would an engineer designing a space exposed to water, heat, fire, moisture, and food choose compressed wood as the ideal material?

The answer is obvious.

## Part Three — Why such a contradiction then?

Wood was nature's fuel.

For centuries, humans burned wood to create fire. Yet today we place wood directly around cooking zones. Kitchen is the only place in our home, where there is fire.

The very material traditionally used to create the fire is now being used to surround the fire.

All Compressed Wood boards absorb moisture.

They swell. They deteriorate. They attract fungus. They can harbor bacteria.

Most ply boards, MDF, HDF, HDHMR, particle boards, contain chemical binders that releases volatile organic compounds (VOCs) over time. That too, in the very place where families prepare food.

This is not material science. This is material compromise.

What History Already Knew

Less than a century ago, kitchens across many parts of the world were built primarily from stone and its allied materials.

Stone was trusted because it was naturally: water resistant, fire resistant, highly durable, hygienic, long lasting.

Stone survives generations. Look at our historical temple and buildings like Taj Mahal — all built in stones.

Stone was always the material nature intended for demanding environments like kitchen where water, heat, fire and fumes work together.

The challenge was never the performance of Stone. The challenge was manufacturing technical capabilities and commercial viability. Stone has always been 5-7 times more expensive than compressed wood, and moreover stone was difficult to engineer, difficult to fabricate, difficult to assemble, and difficult to commercialise at scale.

So as we entered the age of industrialisation, the factories adopted easier and cheaper materials, like compressed wood. Not necessarily better materials. Simply easier and cheaper materials.
